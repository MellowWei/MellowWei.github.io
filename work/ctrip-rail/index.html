01 / Overview

Frame the system before drawing the screen.

Context

During major Chinese travel peaks, people make consequential rail decisions under severe time pressure. They compare schedules and seat inventory, monitor waitlists, coordinate station arrival, and repeatedly check gates, seats, and departure status. The existing Ctrip flow distributed these tasks across dense pages and multiple jumps.

Challenge

Removing information indiscriminately would make the system less safe. The redesign had to preserve essential operational detail while changing its hierarchy over time: before purchase, during selection, after booking, near departure, and while the train was moving.

My role

As an interaction design intern, I audited the end-to-end rail experience, synthesized user feedback and key pain points, decomposed high-traffic pages, mapped the journey, explored more than twenty high-fidelity directions, and iterated three key journey moments with the product team.

Design question

How might we reduce decision effort across the full rail journey while preserving the information travelers need to feel in control?

02 / Design thinking

Evidence → insight → design decision.

I treated the product as one travel service rather than a set of disconnected screens. The audit followed the traveler through route selection, results, seat choice, passenger information, payment and queueing, ticket issue, order review, station arrival, boarding, in-trip progress, recovery, and arrival. Key-page analysis and user feedback were then mapped against what the traveler was trying to accomplish at each stage.

Synthesis

What the evidence changed.

01

The interface made the primary task compete with the page

Travel modes, promotions, tools, and ticket information carried similar visual weight. Users had to rediscover the booking entry and reconstruct the hierarchy on every visit.

02

A train is a bundle of constraints

Travelers evaluate time, duration, seats, price, transfers, station, and probability together. A time-only list and hidden filters force repeated comparison work.

03

Peak demand turns choice into uncertainty

When direct trains sell out, a waitlist alone does not resolve the journey. People need feasible transfer and alternative-train combinations, visible likelihood, and a clear path to act.

04

The ticket has different jobs over time

A static order page cannot serve the day before departure, the final thirty minutes, the train ride, and a missed-train emergency equally well.

05

Ambient access becomes critical near departure

The source research found users checked the order page more than three times in the hour before departure. Gate, seat, status, and remaining time needed to escape the app’s navigation burden.

03 / Strategy

Treat the ticket as a service that changes with time.

The redesign follows three goals across the journey. Before purchase, simplify page structure and make the booking entry unmistakable. During selection, improve decision efficiency through visible status, intelligent ordering, and viable alternatives. After purchase, let a dynamic ticket reorganize itself around departure, boarding, travel, and recovery.

01

Task-first hierarchy

Give the rail task a stable visual anchor and reduce the weight of promotions and secondary tools around it.

02

Constraint-aware choice

Bring common filters forward and compare availability, duration, price, probability, and transfers in the same decision surface.

03

State over screens

Reorganize the ticket by temporal state instead of asking the user to navigate the same static order page repeatedly.

04

Recovery in context

Detect a likely missed train and offer high-confidence alternatives in a lightweight overlay with one-step action.

05

Ambient reassurance

Move the minimum critical status to the lock screen so travelers can confirm progress without reopening the app.

04 / Interface showcase

Product UI, separated from the reasoning.

The final design is a connected system, not one redesigned screen. It begins by cleaning the entry point, continues through direct and no-direct search scenarios, then shifts the order page through pre-departure, departure-day, in-transit, recovery, and lock-screen states.

01 / Purchase home

One screen, one unmistakable booking task

The redesign lowers the hierarchy of other travel modes, separates domestic and international rail choices, strengthens the destination-and-date module, and changes the generic ‘Search’ action into a clear rail-ticket query.

  • Rail entry receives first visual priority
  • Promotions and tools move below the core task
  • Spacing and grouping reduce scanning interference
02 / Direct routes

Make status and frequent controls visible before comparison starts

The results page brings common filters to the top, reinforces departure time and seat availability, and uses color and language to distinguish available, waitlist, and unavailable states.

  • Frequent filters moved into the primary control area
  • Stronger focus on time, seats, and price
  • Intelligent ordering informed by history and stated preference
03 / No direct route

Replace a dead end with comparable alternatives

When direct inventory is exhausted, the interface places train-transfer and multimodal options alongside the original route. Recommended alternatives use the same visual structure so users can compare time, price, transfer burden, and likelihood without leaving the page.

  • Direct and transfer options in one view
  • Recommended alternatives labeled by feasibility
  • Waitlist probabilities presented as decision support
04 / Pre-departure

A low-noise ticket for the day before travel

Twenty-four hours before departure, the page prioritizes the departure countdown, weather, station, gate, time, seat, passenger, and change/refund status. Secondary journey services remain available but no longer compete with the next task.

  • Time-driven hierarchy
  • Critical information proactively surfaced
  • Lower cognitive load for repeated checks
05 / Departure day

Turn the order into a live departure instrument

On the day of travel, the ticket adds weather, station-arrival guidance, a prominent gate, quick access to station maps and services, and an expandable pre-departure state as the clock runs down.

  • Location-aware arrival reminder
  • Gate and station guidance given top priority
  • Compact cards preserve scanability under stress
06 / In transit

Show progress, then intervene only when action is needed

During the trip, a live route timeline shows current station, remaining time, and upcoming stops. If the system detects that the traveler may miss the train, a floating alert offers a one-tap change or waitlist option without forcing a multi-page recovery flow.

  • Live route progress and remaining time
  • Contextual missed-train detection
  • One-step recovery with customer-support access
07 / Lock screen

Critical progress without reopening the app

Compact lock-screen cards surface the trip differently across waiting, security, and in-transit states. Departure and arrival, gate, seat, boarding progress, next stop, and remaining time remain visible in the smallest useful form.

  • State-specific live activity
  • Immediate access to gate and seat
  • Progressive route feedback during travel

05 / Outcome

The redesigned flow improved both business and experience indicators.

+2%booking conversion
−18%task time
−15%page-exit rate
+20%user satisfaction

According to the original project report, post-launch data improved across conversion, task efficiency, page retention, and satisfaction. The largest design contribution was the dynamic-ticket system: it extended the product beyond a purchase confirmation and connected the traveler’s changing information needs across the full journey.

Reflection

Clarity is not less information. It is the right information at the moment it can change a decision.

This project moved my practice from page-level optimization toward journey-level service design. In a complex, high-pressure business, the interface has to balance transparency with operational simplicity and treat time as a primary input to information architecture.

What I learned
  1. Decompose the journey across pre-purchase, purchase, waiting, station, in-trip, and recovery states before optimizing any single page.
  2. Find the decision-simplification point inside complex business logic instead of hiding necessary detail.
  3. Use dynamic hierarchy and ambient surfaces to reduce repeated checking without removing user control.
Next iteration
  1. Extend the dynamic ticket across multimodal journeys such as metro, coach, and air-rail connections.
  2. Explore stronger peak-season fallback and fault-tolerance mechanisms.
  3. Document the validation methodology more completely so product impact can be audited, not only reported.

01 / Overview

Frame the system before drawing the screen.

Context

Most wellbeing products in the source portfolio focused on meditation, habit routines, mood tracking, or symptom management. The project began from a different observation: people also seek a language for meaning, purpose, freedom, anxiety, and their place in the world.

Challenge

Philosophy is often abstract, and emotional support is consequential. The design had to make ideas approachable without reducing them to motivational slogans, connect reflection with everyday practice, and maintain clear boundaries between philosophical inquiry, self-help, and professional mental-health care.

My role

I conducted the literature and competitor review, designed and analyzed the survey, interviewed five participants, developed personas and a journey map, defined the information architecture, illustrated the interface, and produced the high-fidelity concept.

Design question

How might philosophical inquiry become a usable, continuing practice for personal growth—while keeping interpretation with the user and mental-health boundaries visible?

02 / Design thinking

Evidence → insight → design decision.

The research combined desk research with primary user evidence. The literature review explored philosophical reflection, meaning, and wellbeing; a competitor audit examined Headspace, Bloom, and Sanvello; an online survey tested interest and interaction preferences; and five interviews traced how people seek support, interpret difficult experiences, and approach philosophical ideas.

Interview voice · meaning

“I often feel alone in the journey toward better mental health.”

One participant described how difficult it was to find people willing to discuss questions such as meaning and purpose alongside lived experience.

Interview voice · access

“Philosophy can resist some of the emptiness, but it is difficult to enter.”

Participants connected contemporary pressure with meaninglessness while also describing philosophical language as complex and hard to apply without guidance.

Interview voice · translation

“I want complex ideas broken into approachable material.”

A recurring request was to connect philosophical concepts with familiar wellbeing questions and everyday situations rather than present them as abstract doctrine.

01

Literature review

Examine how existential reflection, meaning, practical wisdom, and reflective practice may relate to wellbeing, while avoiding a causal claim that the product itself can treat mental illness.

02

Competitive analysis

Compare meditation, daily-plan, cognitive-behavioral, mood-tracking, and community patterns to identify both mature conventions and the missing philosophical layer.

03

Online survey

Collect 86 responses about interest in philosophy and mental health, barriers to reflection, preferred resources, and expectations for interaction with an AI philosopher.

04

In-depth interviews

Interview five people about the experience of seeking meaning and help, the difficulty of abstract concepts, and whether philosophical dialogue could support personal growth.

Synthesis

What the evidence changed.

01

People wanted orientation, not only relief

Participants described a need to understand what an experience means, why it matters, and how it relates to the kind of life they are trying to build.

02

Interest in philosophy exceeded access to it

Survey and interview participants were open to philosophy, yet complex language and a lack of practical entry points made it difficult to use in everyday life.

03

Reflection needs scaffolding

An abstract idea becomes useful when the system connects it to a lived dilemma, asks a tractable question, and offers a next step without pretending there is one correct interpretation.

04

Continuity matters more than a one-off answer

Participants needed a way to revisit earlier moods, questions, readings, and interpretations so growth could become visible without turning reflection into a performance score.

05

Community can reduce intellectual isolation

People valued discussion around shared questions, workshops, and support groups when the space preserved context and did not optimize primarily for engagement volume.

03 / Strategy

Make philosophy a path of inquiry—not a diagnosis.

The experience was organized around three connected systems: philosophical exploration for meaning, wellbeing tools for emotional continuity, and community for companionship. The AI layer should introduce perspectives and questions, not decide what the user’s experience means or claim therapeutic authority.

01

Questions before answers

Begin with the user’s lived dilemma and use follow-up questions to widen interpretation rather than deliver a verdict.

02

Progressive depth

Offer simple entry points, then connect topics to traditions, thinkers, articles, and longer dialogue as curiosity grows.

03

Reflective continuity

Link conversations, mood check-ins, insights, and saved material without collapsing the person into a single wellbeing score.

04

Visible care boundaries

Separate philosophical exploration from professional care and make escalation, external support, and uncertainty legible.

04 / Interface showcase

Product UI, separated from the reasoning.

The concept turns the research into a four-part experience. Users can enter through a topic or dilemma, move toward philosopher-inspired dialogue and source material, connect reflection with mood and daily practice, and continue through community and a personal record.

01 / Wisdom home

Enter through a lived question

The home surface combines daily wisdom, topic cards, reading recommendations, philosopher-guided conversations, and a visible history of prior exploration. The hierarchy makes philosophy browseable without presenting it as a course catalogue.

  • Everyday questions as entry points
  • Daily wisdom connected to deeper sources
  • Conversation history preserved for continuity
02 / Knowledge structure

Move from topic to tradition without losing the person

The topic library organizes metaphysics, epistemology, ethics, existentialism, philosophy of mind, language, time, and science. Search and community surfaces let users move between structured knowledge and lived discussion.

  • Progressive topic depth
  • Search across conversations, events, and people
  • Community discussions connected to source topics
03 / Dialogic AI

Perspectives, prompts, and a return to the user’s judgment

Philosopher-inspired agents translate a tradition into questions and perspectives. The interaction is designed as guided inquiry: a voice interface can make it approachable, while the product must clearly signal that the agent is interpretive scaffolding—not the historical thinker and not a therapist.

  • Contextual follow-up questions
  • Explicitly plural perspectives
  • Voice interaction for a lower-friction reflective ritual
04 / Wellbeing continuity

Track change without grading the self

Mood check-ins, daily prompts, meditation content, and a personal record help users notice patterns over time. The interface avoids positioning one score as the truth of the person’s psychological state.

  • Mood and reflection history
  • Daily prompts and meditation resources
  • Personalized recommendations with room for user correction
05 / Community support

Turn private reflection into intellectual companionship

Workshops, topic communities, recommended conversations, and support groups extend reflection beyond the individual. Participation is organized around shared questions and context rather than a generic engagement feed.

  • Topic-based community spaces
  • Workshops and guided activities
  • Support groups adjacent to—not substituted by—AI

05 / Outcome

A coherent concept that makes the product’s promise—and its risks—more concrete.

The final design integrates philosophical dialogue, topic exploration, personal records, mood reflection, wellbeing resources, community, and voice interaction. The new embedded prototype turns those static screens into a complete, reproducible journey from state check-in to a user-authored Reflection Trace. It demonstrates interaction logic—not therapeutic efficacy—and makes epistemic and clinical boundaries visible inside the flow.

Evidence boundaryThe portfolio reports an 86-person survey, five interviews, and an eight-week design process. It does not report usability-test results, clinical outcomes, or a live product launch. MindScape should therefore be evaluated as a research-informed concept—not as evidence that philosophy or the interface improves mental health.

Reflection

A system can support interpretation without owning it.

MindScape made the complexity of reflective products visible to me. Accessibility is not only simpler language; it is the ability to connect an idea to a lived situation, preserve uncertainty, offer a responsible next step, and leave the final interpretation with the person.

What I learned
  1. Balance philosophical depth with progressive disclosure and concrete entry points.
  2. Treat AI-generated interpretation as contestable scaffolding, not psychological truth.
  3. Design the relationship between private reflection, community, and professional support—not only isolated features.
Next iteration
  1. Test comprehension and cognitive load across the four-part information architecture.
  2. Add explicit crisis, clinical-support, source-provenance, and AI-identity boundaries.
  3. Improve recommendation controls so users can inspect and correct the system’s interpretation of them.

01 / Overview

Frame the system before drawing the screen.

Context

As an active participant in city events, I repeatedly missed exhibitions, markets, and interest-based gatherings because information was spread across more than twenty public accounts. Small merchants and independent organizers faced the inverse problem: strong events remained invisible outside an existing follower base.

Challenge

The product had to serve both demand and supply. It needed to make a new app useful before it had dense inventory, keep event information current, establish trust for informal gatherings, and avoid becoming another channel that organizers could not maintain.

My role

I independently framed the opportunity, planned stakeholder research, conducted contextual interviews, analyzed competing channels, defined the product strategy and information architecture, and created the high-fidelity mobile concept.

Design question

How might we help residents move from ‘something looks interesting’ to a confident local plan—while giving small organizers a credible, low-effort distribution channel?

02 / Design thinking

Evidence → insight → design decision.

The project documents a two-sided research plan rather than a single consumer study. For residents, the key questions were discovery habits, trust, willingness to adopt a new app, and high-frequency use contexts. For merchants and organizers, the study focused on reach, publishing effort, current workarounds, and what would make a new channel worth maintaining.

Persona synthesis · spontaneous

17 local-life apps installed; only three used weekly.

The source portfolio uses this pattern to represent a high-choice, low-reliability environment: the user still opened six public accounts for a Saturday plan, found expired listings and distant options, then abandoned the search.

Persona synthesis · planning

Eight parenting accounts pinned; 60% of the content felt promotional.

The planning-parent archetype spent a reported 47 minutes browsing in a week, only to discover that a suitable event was already full. Inventory without availability and age-fit signals still creates decision waste.

Persona synthesis · organizing

Seven group reposts still produced repeated questions and late cancellations.

The organizer archetype lacked a current source of truth and a commitment mechanism. This shifted the design toward real-time status, organizer reputation, and a refundable attendance deposit concept.

Synthesis

What the evidence changed.

01

Discovery was not a content-volume problem

People already encountered event posts. The work was reconstructing whether the event was current, nearby, available, affordable, and appropriate for the moment they had.

02

Intent changes with the outing

A Saturday plan with children, a last-minute solo outing, and an interest-group meetup demand different filters, confidence signals, and levels of planning.

03

Every existing channel trades one failure for another

Social feeds offer vivid first-hand accounts but fragmented facts; review platforms are easier to compare but can bury events; private groups feel direct but are costly to manage and easy to miss.

04

Freshness and trust are marketplace infrastructure

A two-sided product cannot rely on aggregation alone. Organizer credibility, current availability, transparent details, and lightweight contribution all determine whether the system remains useful.

03 / Strategy

Answer the decision before asking for commitment.

The strategy reframed CitySeed from ‘a place containing every event’ into a decision-support layer. The product should help someone identify a suitable option in seconds, understand why it fits the present situation, and see enough evidence to act—while returning useful audience signals to organizers.

01

Fast answers

Locate an event type in three seconds, complete a complex filter in one action, and judge basic fit at a glance.

02

Situational relevance

Combine interest with distance, available time, companions, cost, and willingness to plan.

03

Visible trust

Expose organizer identity, current status, participant experience, practical details, and the source of each recommendation.

04

Reusable contribution

Turn the attendee’s on-site notes into practical local knowledge for the next person without demanding a long review.

01 / Intent model

Guidance before the feed

A lightweight onboarding flow collects interests and high-frequency outing scenarios. Instead of asking users to build an exhaustive profile, it captures the conditions that change recommendation quality.

  • Interest clusters for an initial model
  • Scenario prompts for family, solo, group, or spontaneous plans
  • Editable signals rather than a fixed identity
02 / Exploration modes

Browse by theme—or by where the day can take you

Themed zones package activities around recurring intentions, while the aggregation page and map support open exploration. The same inventory can therefore serve both purposeful and spatial discovery.

  • Themed clusters for recurring intentions
  • Map mode for proximity and route planning
  • Shared filter logic across list and map
03 / Adaptive discovery

A feed that learns from decisions, not only clicks

The feed combines explicit preferences with viewed categories, saved events, completed visits, and contribution history. Recommendation cards foreground the details that determine action rather than engagement alone.

  • Decision-critical metadata on every card
  • Preference correction through saves and attendance
  • Serendipity preserved through controlled variety
04 / Contextual AI

Ask the practical question the listing forgot

AI question prompts translate vague hesitation into event-specific answers—whether an activity fits a child, how much time it requires, what to bring, or whether poor weather changes the plan.

  • Suggested questions based on event context
  • Answers grounded in structured listing details
  • Related media and next actions kept on the same page
05 / Contribution loop

Capture useful evidence while the experience is fresh

On-site prompts make contribution small and specific. Attendees can log practical notes, ratings, photos, and recommendations, then turn those fragments into a reusable guide for other residents.

  • Low-friction check-in and structured prompts
  • Practical notes instead of generic sentiment
  • Post-event draft assembled from captured material
06 / Community continuity

Let local knowledge accumulate around people

Profiles, messages, saved events, activity records, and contribution history make discovery cumulative. The community layer supports follow-up without forcing every interaction into a public feed.

  • Messages and activity notifications
  • Collections and visit history
  • Contribution identity and lightweight reputation

05 / Outcome

A complete service concept—and a clearer definition of its real value.

The final system connects discovery, situational recommendation, event detail, map planning, participation, contribution, and community. The project’s strongest shift was conceptual: CitySeed is not valuable because it contains more events; it is valuable when it lowers the labor between noticing an event and deciding to act, while giving organizers better-quality visibility.

Evidence boundaryThis is an independent concept project. The source portfolio documents research framing, design synthesis, and a high-fidelity prototype; it does not report a live launch, usability-test sample, or verified business impact. Early figures presented in the original concept overview are therefore not treated here as outcomes.

Reflection

The aggregator is not the product. The product is a better relationship between people, local information, and action.

CitySeed taught me to move beyond a feature checklist and ask how an ecosystem sustains trust over time. A useful two-sided product has to make the resident’s decision easier and the organizer’s participation worthwhile at the same time.

What I learned
  1. Treat information freshness, organizer identity, and contribution quality as core product architecture.
  2. Model situational intent instead of assuming a single stable interest profile.
  3. Keep speculative market value separate from evidence generated by research or testing.
Next iteration
  1. Run task-based usability tests across spontaneous and planned discovery scenarios.
  2. Prototype the organizer publishing and update workflow, which is underdeveloped in this version.
  3. Validate cold-start supply, trust signals, and AI recommendation rules before claiming adoption value.
